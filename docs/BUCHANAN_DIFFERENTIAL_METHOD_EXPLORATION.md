## 1. Purpose

This exploration examines Ian Buchanan’s articulation of Deleuze and Guattari’s “differential method,” primarily as presented in his 2022 lecture “Deleuze and Guattari’s Differential Method” (delivered at the Institute of Philosophy, Czech Academy of Sciences). The aim is to assess its relevance to the Buchanan Deleuze Intelligence Platform’s goals: observing how meaning is produced through the movement, texture, density, and force of philosophical language, without reductive propositional capture or interpretive overreach.

Buchanan positions the differential method as essential for rigorous reading of Deleuze and Guattari — particularly their assemblage theory — emphasizing specific causalities, qualitative differences, flows, interruptions, and desire over additive connections, extrinsic generalities, or representational fixes.

## 2. Core Thesis of the Differential Method (per Buchanan)

To be a good reader of Deleuze and Guattari, one must apply their differential method. This method:

- Focuses on **specific causalities** rather than general or extrinsic ones (psychological, sociological, or “everything is connected” claims). Invoking overly broad causalities “is as good as saying nothing.”
- Analyzes **assemblages as apparatuses of capture and extraction** that engage specific flows (of desire, matter, images, attention, etc.).
- Distinguishes types of assemblages and flows through **qualitative differences** and internal tensions/agonistics, rather than material similarities or additive accumulation.
- Treats flows as becoming ideal/continuous precisely through **interruption and subtraction**, not addition. “Far from being the opposite of continuity the break or interruption conditions this continuity it presupposes or defines what it cuts into as an ideal continuity.”
- Insists that **all assemblages are assemblages of desire**; affect and intensities are central, not secondary.

Buchanan critiques certain post-Deleuzian assemblage theories (e.g., additive models of distributed agency) for flattening tensions, ignoring cutting edges, and losing the metamorphic, differential character of the original project.

## 3. Key Mechanics and Contrasts

**Flows and Interruptions**:
- Assemblages do not merely connect; they cut into, divert, exploit, or render continuous specific flows.
- Example (Buchanan): Pre-social media, photographs existed in discontinuous folders/hard drives. Uploading to platforms subtracted those barriers, creating a continuous “feed” flow of images — while the assemblage extracts surplus attention/value. The flow appears additive (“and and and”) but is produced by subtraction.
- Flows become “ideal” at the point of interruption; continuity is conditioned by the break.

**Qualitative Differentiation (Weapons vs. Tools example from *A Thousand Plateaus*)**:
- Materially similar objects (e.g., axes) are differentiated not by substance but by the assemblages and affects they enter: weapons (projection, speed, war machine, exteriority) versus tools (interception, preparation for equilibrium, interiority, house).
- The differential method traces these qualitative distinctions and their specific effects, rather than reducing to shared materiality or general “technology.”

**Subtraction over Addition**:
- Multiplicities and flows are often created or revealed through subtraction (removing discontinuities, barriers, or extraneous elements) rather than piling on connections. Additive models risk “analytic defeat” by making everything equivalent and connected without cuts or tensions.

**Desire and Affect**:
- Desire is productive and assembling; there is “no desire but assembling assembled desire.”
- Assemblages involve capture of flows for specific effects; affect circulates as pure intensities enabling ways of being.

**Contrast with Other Approaches**:
- Vs. Dialectical or totalizing methods: Avoids homogenizing sums; preserves internal agonistics and specific effects.
- Vs. Representational or idealist readings: Flows are ideal but practiced through concrete interruptions and social machines; the method is not abstract representation.
- Vs. Additive/distributed agency models: Rejects flattening into pure connectivity; insists on bounding, cutting, and tracing specific causalities and lines of flight.

## 4. Relation to Deleuze & Guattari’s Project and Buchanan’s Corpus

The differential method is the analytic complement to assemblage theory. It rethinks Freudian complexes via desiring-machines (*Anti-Oedipus*) and extends into practical social/cultural analysis. Buchanan’s broader work — *Deleuzism: A Metacommentary*, *Assemblage Theory and Method*, essays on schizoanalysis, space, and affect — consistently applies this method: mapping concepts onto real assemblages while preserving their differential, intensive, and metamorphic character.

It aligns with schizoanalytic cartographies (Guattari) and the emphasis on lines of flight, deterritorialization, and experimentation. Buchanan presents it as a tool for nomadic thought: finding outsides, tracing becomings, and avoiding capture by reductive frameworks.

## 8. Conceptual Fidelity

Buchanan’s differential method does not offer a technique for mastering or fixing meaning in Deleuze and Guattari. It offers instruments for following the specific movements, interruptions, qualitative distinctions, and desiring flows through which their concepts operate and transform. In the context of this platform, it enlarges the capacity to observe the production of meaning through textual density, recursion, drift, and intensity — while insisting that such observation remain experimental, specific, human-governed, and always open to further differentiation.

No reductive claim is made that computational proxies or interface features replicate Buchanan’s method. They may support attentive, differential exploration of the philosophical corpus the platform exists to make inspectable.
